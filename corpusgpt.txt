Throughout history, numerous indigenous communities have endured oppression from governments that purport to act in their best interests.
Oftentimes, when this occurs, the oppressed do not simply endure oppression and may rebel, sometimes gaining de facto independence from their oppressors.
One notable example is the Zapatistas, a coalition formed by several indigenous groups in Chiapas, Mexico.
They have long endured oppression, including the erosion of land rights and the implementation of other racist policies.
This provoked an uprising among Chiapas’s indigenous peoples, and on the day the NAFTA agreement was signed, the Zapatistas declared themselves an independent state.
Conflict within the region unleashed a sweeping military response from the Mexican government, as the Zapatistas kidnapped the governor and coerced him into hard labor, while the government's peace initiatives proved hollow and a succession of tragic, horrific events—including a state-led massacre—followed.
The government's resort to violence stemmed from a deliberate aim to quell any form of rebellion that might threaten its grip on power.
This era marked a deliberate effort to project Mexico onto the global stage as a strong, capable nation, a period of substantial growth that finally dispelled the notion of it being a third-world country.
Although considerable efforts have aimed to secure regional peace over the past thirty years, lasting tranquility remains unattained, largely because the Mexican government has avoided addressing the underlying grievances that gave rise to the Zapatista uprising.
Such rebellions are by no means unique; history is full of examples of indigenous groups uniting to resist oppression, which lends credence to the legitimacy of these movements.
All told, the Zapatistas’ struggle for peace and security persists, yet it is continually intensified by the actions of the Mexican government.
For more than two millennia, the Maya people of Chiapas have thrived by tending the land, preserving its health, and sustaining their homeland.
Yet in 1523, their peace and prosperity were shattered as the colonizer Luis Marín began to conquer the region.
He managed to seize land inhabited by many indigenous communities, but his campaigns against the Tzotzil people encountered substantial difficulties (Schmal).
Fortune did not permit them to conquer all of the land; nevertheless, the Spaniards refused to abandon their goal of conquering all Maya lands and continued fighting.
By 1528, the land had been completely subdued, and the last remnants of indigenous peoples who remained free were now under the control of the Spanish Crown.
The Spanish established a system of enslaving Indigenous peoples, a policy that further eroded their rights as inhabitants of the land until it was abolished.
Even though slavery had come to an end, the Spaniards— and, subsequently, the Mexicans— remained at odds with the indigenous peoples.
Historically, the indigenous peoples of Chiapas have been excluded from participation in the government's decision‑making processes and from enjoying basic human rights and essential services such as education and healthcare (Godelmann).
Moreover, the government sought to seize land by fabricating a scenario in which it merely resolved disputes between different indigenous groups (Krinsky), and it ended up confiscating all the land, ignoring the many other indigenous communities that were not part of the Lacandon Community.
As Krinsky notes, the Spanish and Mexican authorities have long subjected the people of Chiapas to extraordinary injustice, fostering deep-seated resentment that would eventually catalyze a liberation movement.
The simmering tensions in the region surrounding the indigenous population finally boiled over on New Year’s Day, 1994 (Mora).
That day was expected to mark Mexico's entry into the North American Free Trade Agreement, and, as a result, to alter the global perception of the country so that it would no longer be regarded as a third-world nation.
Originating with the Zapatista Army of National Liberation (EZLN)—known in English as the Zapatista National Liberation Army—the moment was defined by its declaration of war against the Mexican state.
It was not merely performative: the Zapatistas briefly occupied six towns in the state's central highlands, before retreating to the jungle to avoid further conflict (Harvey, Arnsen, 129).
Fighting continued, and escalating tensions with the Mexican military compelled the Zapatistas to resort to increasingly brutal means of enforcing their rule, including the kidnapping of Absalón Castellanos Domínguez, the seventy-year-old former Governor of Chiapas (Depalma).
Despite ongoing peace talks, the tension endures to this day, albeit in a milder form, as the Mexican government will not heed the Zapatistas’ demands.
These demands include truly free and democratic elections; the resignation of the incumbent federal executive and the sitting state executives; the recognition of the EZLN as a belligerent force; the end of neoliberal policy; and general elections for the entire state of Chiapas, among many other points (CCRI-CG of the EZLN).
Since these demands have never been met, the EZLN continues to regard itself as a de facto state and remains at odds with the leadership and military forces of the Mexican government.
It is essential to acknowledge that these demands arise from the abuses perpetrated by both the Spanish and Mexican governments, and until the Mexican government addresses its past wrongs, the people of Chiapas will not accept them as a legitimate state and will continue to strive for their own independence.
The cause of all this conflict lies entirely in the actions and inactions of the Mexican government.
Historically, the actions of the Spanish government were egregious, including the enslavement of Indigenous peoples, their displacement from ancestral lands, and a host of other affronts.
Once Mexico secured its independence, it gained the opportunity to address the wrongs inflicted by the Spanish crown upon the indigenous peoples—a fresh start.
That alleged fresh start, however, as previously noted, proved nothing of the sort and, in fact, exacerbated the hardships faced by Indigenous communities.
As we enter the modern era, the present incarnation of government has once more acted against these people.
Although their anger is legitimate and justified, the government brands them as a band of rebellious hoodlums and dismisses their demands as irrelevant and unjustified.
Government policies toward Indigenous peoples are not unique to the state of Mexico; across the United States, Canada, Australia, and numerous other countries, Indigenous communities have endured unfair treatment.
That being said, many wrongs do not constitute a right, and Mexico’s attempt to justify its actions merely because others have managed to get away with them only serves to worsen the situation in Chiapas.
Had the Mexican government addressed its demands in the manner they deserved, not only would regional peace have prevailed and countless lives been saved, but such a course of action would also have demonstrated to the world that Mexico is a capable power, worthy of being regarded as a first‑world nation.
The way the indigenous Maya who inhabit this land are treated reveals to the world the cruelty of the government, a cruelty that persists and seems aimed only at garnering more attention and favorable press for the EZLN.
Had the Mexican government treated these indigenous communities with fairness, such a predicament would never have arisen in Chiapas; and if it had listened to the reasoning put forward by the EZLN, this government crisis would belong to the past rather than to the present.

Although Mexico had appeared relatively strong at the founding of the nation, the twentieth century brought narcotics trafficking, gang activity, and weak economies, and as a result the country was viewed as a Third World nation and was not taken seriously on the global stage.

Following the destruction of the temple, the law took on a markedly more interpretive character and a stronger unity than that observed during the Second Temple era.
The foundations of Jewish law trace back to the biblical era, taking shape during the time of the First Temple.
In that period, the law was perceived as a pedagogical instrument rather than as a strictly statutory framework.
Put simply, laws exist to educate rather than to prescribe an inflexible way of life that one must dutifully follow.
Moreover, for much of the early First Temple period, the Torah was concealed, and as a result the precise laws were not consistently observed; a clear illustration of this is the episode involving King David and the spoils of battle.
During the period of the Second Temple, a pronounced divergence emerged from differing interpretations of the law.

Manzanita Village at UC Santa Barbara stands as a quintessential example, conceived to house eleven hundred UCSB students.
(The 2024 status of UCSB's campus housing)
Completed in 2001, the structure is relatively recent and adopts a minimalist housing philosophy, using its space to maximize occupancy rather than showcasing elaborate, complex designs.
In design practice, uncomplicated geometric shapes such as rectangles and triangles are employed because they are cheaper and more space-efficient than more ornate alternatives.
Additionally, architectural features that offer little or no function—such as ornate pillars or statues—are not used, with every element of Manzanita Village designed to serve practicality rather than the site’s aesthetics.
In brief, Manzanita Village stands as a strong illustration of the trend in contemporary architecture toward prioritizing function over form, a philosophy often labeled functionalism that promises savings in both cost and construction time for modern architectural works.
The design of Manzanita Village was purposefully oriented toward spatial efficiency, expanding the footprint of each building to accommodate more students.
Rather than relying solely on wood or steel, Manzanita Village is primarily constructed from reinforced concrete, with wood and steel used only as minor materials; concrete offers several advantages, including low cost, durability, and the capacity for rooms to cool themselves at night (Bell and Rand).
These advantages can boost occupancy, because lower costs enable more buildings to be constructed, and rooms that can cool themselves at night eliminate the need for separate air-conditioning infrastructure, thereby freeing space and further maximizing capacity.
Additionally, Manzanita Village comprises several buildings arranged in a U-shaped layout, allowing three structures to share a communal lawn rather than each building possessing its own lawn.
Maintaining a single extensive communal lawn would require a substantial portion of the budget to be allocated to irrigation, funds that could otherwise be used for housing.
In functional contemporary architecture, resources are prioritized toward core efficiency and social utility rather than toward superfluous enhancements such as intricate cooling systems or expansive, luxurious lawns.
Additionally, Manzanita Village adopts a minimalist aesthetic, favoring simple geometric forms over elaborate ornamentation.
Functionalism remains a foundational element of contemporary architecture, while current practice rejects the notion that elaborate intricacy alone determines beauty in architecture.
Since the industrial revolution, emphasis has shifted toward function at the expense of form, yielding simpler designs that prioritize practicality over spectacle (Giedion).
Within Manzanita Village, the rectangle emerges as the predominant geometric form, defining not only the buildings themselves but also the windows, their shades, and the arrangement of the rooms inside.
Triangles are likewise employed in construction, since they represent the essential geometric form that underpins roof design.
These forms supply essential structural integrity, and the more refined designs do likewise; instead of becoming convoluted, their beauty resides in the simplicity of the design.
In designing this housing complex, one might have embraced elaborate designs, yet the designer chose a simple, functional geometry, prioritizing practicality over a flashy aesthetic.
In contemporary architecture, beauty typically arises from simple, elegantly arranged forms—the so‑called decorated shed—rather than from intricate shapes that are costly and inefficient.
Moreover, every element of Manzanita Village carries functional significance alongside its visual appeal, a defining hallmark of contemporary architecture.
Many older architectural works contain ornamental details—such as fountains and statues—that contribute nothing to the building’s function, a trait that is seldom found in decorated-sheds, the very type of functional buildings used for Manzanita Village.
As noted earlier, Manzanita Village comprises several housing buildings for students, multiple lawns where students socialize, and a network of paths that connect the buildings to one another and to the broader UCSB campus.
Each element of every building—and the village as a whole—contributes to the overall functionality, whether through a roof pitched to direct rain toward the grass to irrigate the crops, or through colored awnings that shade windows and curb the intrusion of excess light.
In the older idiom of architecture, a multitude of ornamental and stylistic features often augmented the work's allure, even though they did little to improve its practical functionality.
Several elements at Manzanita Village are designed to be aesthetically pleasing while simultaneously supporting the housing complex’s practical functionality.
Manzanita Village serves as a compelling example of how contemporary architecture can fuse visual allure with practical functionality in a built form.
To conclude, architecture’s long history has been shaped by many trends, and among contemporary practice one notable direction is functionalism—the minimalist commitment to prioritizing function over form, while still accounting for aesthetics within the built works.
Manzanita Village at UCSB, built in the 21st century, stands as a contemporary architectural work that recalls a functionalist mindset: occupancy was maximized by minimizing material costs and by leveraging the surrounding environment—rather than air conditioning—to cool the building.
Moreover, simple geometric shapes were preferred to more elaborate forms that demanded more space and higher construction costs.
Moreover, every aspect of the architectural design contributes to the functionality of the buildings, and nothing is built purely for aesthetic purposes.
Where earlier currents in architecture sought to adorn form with ornate detailing in the hope of beauty, contemporary practice—exemplified by works such as Manzanita—argues that beauty can reside in architecture that is essentially functional.
Viewed as a whole, Manzanita Village exemplifies a prevailing trend in contemporary architecture: prioritizing functionality with cost-efficient designs that meet the building's needs without sacrificing its aesthetic appeal.

In their 1986 paper, Herbert Clark and Deanna Wilkes-Gibbs describe an experiment in which two participants were tasked with solving a tangram puzzle without being able to see what the other was doing.
The experiment described in this paper rests on the foundational work of Clark and Wilkes-Gibbs.
For this study, the experiment presented every participant with an identical tangram puzzle, and the time required for each individual to place all pieces correctly was recorded.
This study sought to determine whether, as participants became more familiar with the tangram during the experiment, the overall time required to solve it would decrease—starting from the initial attempt, when the puzzle was new to both participants—or whether the solving time would remain constant.
Duration serves as a proxy for sociolectal development: as participants converge on a shared sociolect, dialogue becomes more streamlined, and speech tends to be shortened.
In the experiment, the two partners, referred to as A and M, were allocated either a single image displaying tangram pieces in a fixed sequence, or a collection of tangram pictures to arrange independently.
There were three trials in total, and in each, the roles of the person giving instructions and the one sorting the pictures were swapped: in the first trial, A gave instructions and M sorted the pictures, and they reversed roles for the second trial. Throughout the trials, the puzzle pieces were identical, differing only in their orientation.
Participants’ viewpoints were deliberately obscured, preventing them from seeing what the other person saw and forcing them to rely solely on vocal cues to communicate how to orient the puzzle pieces.
Participants were told that their actions would be recorded, but they were not informed about how the recordings would be used.
Following the trial, the recording was transcribed, and the duration of each puzzle piece was recorded for every trial. Although the timing of the first pass was noted, these data were not used in the results, since the experiment aimed to determine whether further development occurred between turn two and turn three.
The results of the aforementioned experiment were as follows: between trials two and three, the average time taken for the describer to correctly explain to the matcher the location of each tangram was more than halved.
Moreover, the maximum duration required to obtain a single image decreased from trial two to trial three, dropping from 0:37 to 0:26.
Across all trials, the matcher consistently positioned each tangram with precise accuracy.
During trial 2, several of the shapes had already been given impromptu names that originated in trial 1—such as “eroding IV cliff”—and by trial 3, most tangrams had been assigned names and/or identities shared by both participants, resulting in little to no confusion on the part of the matcher about who the describer was referring to.
Ultimately, the experiment's findings indicate that even after participants had seen and heard every image described once, a further development of the sociolect of A and M occurred.
Drawing on Clark and Wilkes-Gibbs, it is well established that the number of conversational turns tends to diminish with each successive trial, a pattern observable in the general population; however, our experiment demonstrates that even a well-established couple’s sociolect can still be refined and expanded under new circumstances.
This supports the notion that sociolects are in continual flux, whereas a non-evolving, already developed sociolect would likely yield nearly identical average times for describing pictures, particularly after all images have been described once and participants become comfortable with both the visuals and the experimental procedure.
Although the results are intriguing, this study is hampered by a major limitation: examining only a single couple provides a sample size too small to draw definitive conclusions.
That being said, a similar experiment should be conducted to evaluate how strong this effect is across several different types of relationships—co-workers, a boss and employee, a couple, and two randomly selected strangers—and to determine which relationship type exhibits the greatest decrease in average time from trial two to trial three.
Taken together, these findings indicate that sociolects—or the language varieties shaped by social affinity—continue to evolve over time, even among people who know each other well, in response to new circumstances and shifting conversational topics.
